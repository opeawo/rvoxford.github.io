How to Protect Stored Vehicles During Atlanta Storms

Atlanta's severe weather season brings real risks to stored vehicles. Hail, high winds, flooding, and debris can cause thousands in damage to unprotected vehicles. While you can't control the weather, you can choose appropriate storage and take steps to minimize storm damage risk.

Atlanta's Severe Weather Patterns

Storm Season

Peak period: March through May (spring severe weather season)

Secondary period: September through November (fall transition storms)

Year-round: Isolated thunderstorms can occur any month

What to Expect

Hail: 2-4 significant hail events per year in the Metro area. Hail can range from pea-sized (minor) to golf ball-sized or larger (severe).

Straight-line winds: Thunderstorms often produce 50-70+ mph winds, capable of blowing debris and toppling trees.

Tornadoes: Rare but possible. EF0-EF2 tornadoes occur in the Metro every few years.

Flooding: Flash flooding during heavy rain events. Affects low-lying areas and areas with poor drainage.

Storage Type and Storm Protection

Outdoor Uncovered

Protection level: None against storm damage

Risks:

  • Full hail exposure
  • Direct debris impact
  • Flooding if drainage is poor
  • No protection from any storm element

Mitigation:

  • Comprehensive insurance
  • Car cover (provides minimal cushioning, not real protection)
  • Choose facility with excellent drainage
  • Avoid spaces under/near trees

Covered Storage

Protection level: Good against most storm threats

What it protects against:

  • Hail (complete protection)
  • Moderate debris (roof catches most falling objects)
  • Direct rain

What it doesn't protect against:

  • Flooding
  • Extreme winds (debris can blow in from sides)
  • Tornado-level events

Best covered storage features:

  • Solid, well-maintained roof structure
  • Partial side enclosure (better debris protection)
  • Elevated or well-drained location

Indoor Storage

Protection level: Excellent against most storm threats

What it protects against:

  • Hail (complete)
  • Debris (complete)
  • Rain (complete)
  • Most wind events

What it doesn't protect against:

  • Flooding (if building is in flood zone)
  • Building collapse (extreme events)

Climate-Controlled

Same protection as indoor, plus additional building envelope protection.

Before Storm Season

Choose Storm-Resistant Storage

If you're in outdoor storage and storm season is approaching, consider:

  • Moving to covered or indoor storage for the season
  • Understanding the cost-benefit (one hail event can cost more than months of upgraded storage)

Verify Insurance

Comprehensive coverage: Should cover storm damage including hail

Deductible: Know what you'll pay out of pocket

Coverage limits: Appropriate for your vehicle's value

Claim process: Understand what documentation you'll need

Assess Your Facility

Drainage:

  • Has the facility flooded before?
  • How does water flow during heavy rain?
  • Is your specific space in a low area?

Trees:

  • Are there large trees that could fall or drop branches?
  • Can you request a space away from trees?

Structure condition:

  • For covered/indoor: Is the structure well-maintained?
  • Are there loose panels, damaged roofing, or other vulnerabilities?

When Severe Weather Approaches

With Advance Warning (24+ hours)

If you have flexibility:

  • Consider moving vehicle to more protected storage
  • For outdoor storage, ensure car cover is secured
  • Check that nothing is loose on or around your vehicle
  • Verify battery maintainer is unplugged (surge protection)

Document:

  • Take photos of vehicle condition
  • Note mileage and any existing damage
  • Have insurance info accessible

Short-Notice Storms

If you can't move the vehicle:

  • Ensure it's locked and secured
  • Accept that outdoor vehicles are at risk
  • Have insurance documentation ready

After a Storm

Initial Assessment

Before entering the facility:

  • Check for downed power lines
  • Watch for flooding or unstable structures
  • Take photos of any visible damage to facility

Vehicle Inspection

Exterior:

  • Walk around checking all panels for dents
  • Inspect roof, hood, trunk lid
  • Check glass for cracks
  • Look for debris damage
  • Photograph everything

Underneath:

  • Look for flood evidence (water lines, debris caught)
  • Check for visible damage

Interior (if flooding suspected):

  • Check carpets for moisture
  • Look for water lines on interior surfaces
  • Smell for mold/mildew

If Damage Occurred

  1. Document thoroughly — Photos, video, written notes
  2. Contact insurance — Report promptly
  3. Don't drive until assessing whether it's safe
  4. Get professional assessment if damage is significant
  5. Contact facility — They may have documentation/footage

Flood-Specific Considerations

Never Drive Through Flood Water

If your storage facility is flooded:

  • Wait for water to recede
  • Don't try to retrieve your vehicle from standing water
  • Water damage can be hidden and severe

Flood Damage Reality

Even a few inches of standing water can:

  • Destroy electronics and wiring
  • Ruin interior materials
  • Cause mold that never fully resolves
  • Total a vehicle insurance-wise

Choosing Flood-Resistant Storage

  • Ask if the facility has flooded before
  • Visit after heavy rain to see drainage
  • Choose spaces in elevated areas if possible
  • Avoid facilities in known flood zones
